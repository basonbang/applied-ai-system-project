# Reflection: Profile Comparisons

## High-Energy Pop vs. Chill Lofi

These two profiles are near-opposites. The Pop listener wants energy at 0.9, danceability at 0.85, and doesn't like acoustic sounds. The Lofi listener wants energy at 0.3, moderate danceability, and loves acoustic textures. The results reflect this cleanly — there is zero overlap between their top 5 lists. The Pop listener gets Sunrise City and Gym Hero (loud, upbeat, electronic production), while the Lofi listener gets Library Rain and Midnight Coding (quiet, mellow, acoustic-heavy). This makes sense: the numerical preferences are so far apart that even if a song matched one user's genre, its energy and acousticness scores would tank it for the other user.

## High-Energy Pop vs. Deep Intense Rock

Both profiles want high energy (0.9 and 0.95), but they split on everything else. The Pop listener wants happy mood, high valence (0.8), and high danceability (0.85). The Rock listener wants intense mood, low valence (0.3), and moderate danceability (0.6). The interesting overlap is Gym Hero — it shows up at #2 for Pop and #3 for Rock. Why does a pop song keep appearing for a rock listener? Because Gym Hero has the highest energy in the catalog (0.93) and its mood is "intense," which is exactly what the Rock profile asked for. The system treats Gym Hero like a bridge song — it doesn't sound like rock, but it *feels* like rock in terms of intensity. This is a realistic recommendation a streaming app might make, though a rock purist would probably skip it.

## Chill Lofi vs. All-Zero Numericals

The Lofi profile asks for lofi/chill with low-but-reasonable numbers (energy 0.3, valence 0.6). The All-Zero profile asks for rock/chill with every number at the absolute minimum. You might expect similar results since both want calm music, but the genre difference matters — or rather, it *doesn't* matter as much as you'd think. The All-Zero profile asked for rock, yet got Spacewalk Thoughts (ambient) at #1 instead of any rock song. Both profiles share three of their top 5 songs (Spacewalk Thoughts, Library Rain, Midnight Coding), which tells us that when you push numerical values to extremes, genre becomes almost irrelevant. The scoring math explains this: genre is worth 0.25 points, but the five numerical features together are worth 0.60. When every rock song gets hammered on energy proximity (Storm Runner's 0.91 energy vs. the user's 0.0 target = only a 0.09 score on that feature), even a perfect genre match can't dig out of that hole.

## High-Energy Pop vs. High Energy + Sad Mood (Edge Case)

These profiles share the same genre (pop) and similar energy (0.9), but the edge case flips the mood to "sad" and the valence down to 0.2. The result is that Gym Hero rises from #2 to #1. Why? Sunrise City was #1 for the normal Pop profile because it matched on both genre AND mood (happy). The sad-mood profile can't give Sunrise City that mood bonus anymore, so Gym Hero — which never had the mood bonus in the first place but has slightly better raw energy numbers — takes the lead. Meanwhile, Broken Neon (the emo/sad song) sneaks into #3 purely on its mood match, even though it's not pop and has moderate energy (0.55). This is a great example of how a single preference change can reshuffle the entire list. It also shows the "conflicting vibes" problem: the system can't tell you that "high-energy sad pop" is a contradictory request — it just does the math and gives you a compromise that might not satisfy either desire.

## Deep Intense Rock vs. Non-existent Genre (Edge Case)

The Rock profile gets a clear #1 (Storm Runner at 0.95) because genre and mood both connect. The "country" profile gets a completely flat ranking — no song scores above 0.58, and the top 5 is a random-looking mix of soul, indie pop, pop, folk, and blues. This is what happens when genre contributes zero for every song: you lose 25% of the possible score range, and the remaining features aren't distinctive enough to create clear winners. It's like asking for directions to a city that isn't on the map — the system just points you toward wherever else seems vaguely close. In a real app, this would be a terrible user experience, and you'd want to either tell the user "we don't have country music" or fall back to a different recommendation strategy.

## Chill Lofi vs. High Energy + Sad Mood (Edge Case)

These two profiles have almost nothing in common on paper — one is calm and acoustic, the other is loud and sad. Yet both end up surfacing songs that feel "moody" in different ways. The Lofi user gets peaceful ambient tracks, while the Sad user gets an unexpected mix of upbeat pop (Gym Hero, Sunrise City) alongside dark/sad tracks (Broken Neon, Ghost Signal). The Lofi results feel coherent — you could put them on a playlist and it would flow. The Sad results feel scattered because the system is being pulled in two directions: the genre and energy weights push toward pop bangers, while the mood and valence weights push toward melancholy songs. This comparison highlights that internal consistency matters for good recommendations. A user whose preferences all point in the same direction (like Chill Lofi) gets a focused, satisfying list. A user with contradictions gets a grab bag.
